3 Central tendency

Measures of central tendency is a fancy phrase for ‘average’. They are a single data point used to represent a ‘typical’ value from your data. Depending on your level of measurement you can use one or more measures of central tendency.

3.1 Mode

The most common unique value. For example, 10 people are asked their favourite drink and you have the following results:

##  [1] "espresso"      "latte"         "tea"           "tea"          
##  [5] "earl gray"     "americano"     "hot chocolate" "tea"          
##  [9] "hot chocolate" "tea"

The most common result (mode) is tea, which we can confirm with a quick frequency table:

drinks %>% 
  count(choice) %>% 
  arrange(desc(n))
## # A tibble: 6 x 2
##   choice            n
##   <chr>         <int>
## 1 tea               4
## 2 hot chocolate     2
## 3 americano         1
## 4 earl gray         1
## 5 espresso          1
## 6 latte             1

Mode is the only measure of central tendency you can provide for nominal data.
