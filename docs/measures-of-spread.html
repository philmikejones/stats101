4 Measures of spread

We saw in the measures of central tendency chapter that the mean can be a poor representation of data if the data is skewed, and that we should therefore be careful when someone presents us with a mean (or average) without any further information.

One of the types of ‘further information’ that can help us is a measure of spread of the data around the mean value. We usually use the variance and the standard deviation to quantify measure of spread. Both are easy to calculate, and even easier to convert between each other.

To calculate the variance:

  1. subtract the mean from each score
  2. square the result
  3. sum the results to produce one value
  4. divide by \(n - 1\) (number of observations minus one)

\[ \tag{4.1} \frac{\Sigma (x - \bar{x}) ^ 2}{N-1} \]

var(dat$income)
## [1] 88642982

Using \(n - 1\) rather than simply the number of observations is known as Bessel’s correction.
